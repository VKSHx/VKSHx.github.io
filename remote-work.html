The Remote Work Revolution: Two Years On

By Sarah Chen · May 2026

The shift to distributed work has proven more durable than most predicted. Far from a pandemic anomaly, remote and hybrid arrangements have settled into the operating model of a majority of knowledge-work organisations.

The data tells a nuanced story. Productivity in deep-focus roles has improved on most measures, while roles that depend on spontaneous collaboration show mixed results. The office, it turns out, is better at some things than others.

What changed permanently is the employee-employer relationship around time and place. Workers who once commuted 90 minutes daily are reluctant to give that back without clear evidence of value. Employers who found that output held up during full remote are now negotiating from a weaker position than before.

The companies navigating this best are not those with strict policies in either direction. They are the ones that have been honest about which roles genuinely benefit from in-person presence — and which do not.
